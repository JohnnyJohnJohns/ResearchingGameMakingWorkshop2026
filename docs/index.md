# Research Game Making Workshop at FDG 2026

In recent years there has been significant growth in the volume of scholarly work investigating game making from a variety of perspectives \[2, 4, 7, 8\]. While the multidisciplinarity of this work showcases a breadth of approaches it can also become siloed into disciplinary dialogues \[3, 11\]. For those entering the field there is a lack of awareness of the breadth of approaches and emerging research trends in relation to game making research. Additionally, researching game design and development allows game makers to understand how others have approached similar and/or different design spaces. As part of an artistic practice, this represents an important factor in the development of the field and our role as educators and researchers.


The growth of games making scholarship coincides with an increase in academic game design and development programmes offered in higher education \[6\]. Many of these programmes attempt to bridge the theory of game studies with the craft of game development. However, the approaches educators take are often based on individual academic backgrounds and departmental structures \[1, 5, 6, 9, 10\]. As a result, students may struggle to identify where their research interest in game making fits within the broader research landscape and its relationship to practice.


This workshop proposes to bring together researchers who are interested in understanding and elucidating the game making process. We want to facilitate discussion on what current themes and topics are important, but also what are the most critical gaps in knowledge. It is hoped that by sharing our approaches we can start to develop larger and more ambitious collaborations as well as reach towards methodological strategies that embrace the diversity of approaches employed to study how games are made. The workshop may be of interest to anyone engaged in or interested in researching game making practices, in particular those from game design research, game production studies, games education and related fields.

# Workshop Structure

The half-day workshop will be structured as follows:

•  15 Minutes: Participant introduction and workshop overview
•  30 Minutes: Participants will be invited to present Case Studies as part of their workshop registration. A selection of applications will have an opportunity to provide an overview of an approach they have applied to researching game making
•  30 Minutes: Building a research network activity where participants develop a network of connections between their experiences and topics. Similar to the [_Low-Tech_ _Social_ _Network_](https://gamestorming.com/low-tech-social-network/)
•  15 Minutes: Research network debrief and open discussion
•  30 Minutes: Break
•  45 Minutes: Participants will collaborate to develop a series of research approaches answering methodological questions and assumptions inherent to their approaches through a research jam
•  15 Minutes: Debrief of the research jam
•  30 Minutes: Where to from here? Open discussion about the potential publication/dissemination/formation of a COST action around the topic


If accepted we will develop the outline further and may adjust the structure to remain flexible and encourage a productive dialogue among participants.

# Objectives

The objectives of the workshop are as follows:

•  To gather researchers from differing academic traditions with shared interests in game making
•  To map current themes and topics, as well as challenges in studying game making
•  To share experiences and methodological approaches applied in the study of the game development process
•  To begin to think about how we might structure research methodologies for researching game making
•  To facilitate collaboration and support researchers in the field
•  To look at potential publication and the formation of a COST action around the topic


# Organisers

**John P. Healy** is a Lecturer in game design across undergraduate and postgraduate programmes in the School of Media, TU Dublin. His research explores the creative processes of game designers and the signature pedagogies of game design education. It focuses on how the practice of digital game design unfolds at the interface between computers and culture. He is interested in the areas of game studies, specifically game design research and production studies, as well as how creativity and implementation interact in the construction of digital games.


**Annakaisa Kultima** is a University Lecturer at Aalto University School of Arts, Design and Architecture. She has been publishing over 100 academic papers on game design and development since 2006 and led multiple successful research projects. Kultima’s (2018) PhD dissertation “Game Design Praxiology” examined the multitude of issues of understanding the creative work of game developers spanning a period of 2006-2016 with an ethnography of hundreds of game professionals. Kultima is a long-standing and trusted member of the game ecosystem in Finland and internationally reaching a wide spectrum of practitioners and ecosystem actors.


**Rilla Khaled** is an Associate Professor in the Department of Design and Computation Arts at Concordia University in Montréal, Canada, where she teaches interaction design, design theory, programming, and more. She is the director of the Technoculture, Art and Games (TAG) Research Centre, Canada’s most well-established games research lab, in the Milieux Institute for Arts, Culture, and Technology. Dr. Khaled’s research is focused on the use of interactive technologies to improve the human condition, a career-long passion that has led to diverse outcomes, including designing award-winning serious games, developing a framework for game design specifically aimed at reflective outcomes, creating speculative prototypes of near-future technologies, working with Indigenous communities to use contemporary technologies to imagine new, inclusive futures, and establishing foundations for materials-based game design research.


**Clara Fernández-Vara** is a media scholar, and a game designer and writer. Her work focuses on narrative design, both as her professional practice and in her academic work, as well as videogame history. Clara’s videogame work is grounded in the humanities, informed by her background in literature, film and theatre, which she brings to digital technologies. Before joining the NYU Game Center, Clara spent six years at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology as a researcher and game developer. She holds a Ph.D. in Digital Media from the Georgia Institute of Technology and a Masters in Comparative Media Studies from MIT. Clara has presented her work at various international academic and industry conferences, such as DiGRA (Digital Games Research Association), Foundations of Digital Games, the Electronic Literature Organization Conference, Game Developer’s Conference (GDC), and East Coast Games Conference. She has worked on commercial games for Warner Bros., Die Gute Fabrik, the Spanish National Ballet, and Big Fish Games among others. Her book, _Introduction to Game Analysis_, is now on its third edition.

# Publication Plans


We intend to run apply to run the workshop at DiGRA 2026 in order to capture a variety of perspectives before moving forward with publication plans. The workshop aims to encourage participants to consider the potential publication of the workshop papers in a special issue of ACM Games or to look towards a larger publication as an edited collection. If the workshop is successful we would like to consider the potential to host an annual workshop to build on the network and collaboration. In addition to this, it is hoped that the workshop will lead to the formation of a network and potential COST action around the topic.


# Participation

Participation from a variety of backgrounds is encouraged. Ideally, participants will be interested in the study of how games are made and either have experience in or intentions to research within this area. Researchers, educators and practitioners are encouraged to attend. Participants will be invited to present short prepared case studies (1-3 slide overview of their approach) as part of the workshop.


We envisage a capacity for approximately 15-20 participants but are happy to work with organisers to coordinate this as required. Participants will be asked to submit a brief expression of interest and indicate their desire to present a case study as part of the workshop. We hope that following the workshop participants will have to opportunity to explore initiatives for joint research projects and collaboration.


We would like to support hybrid participation but are conscious of how this can create obstacles in terms of facilitating both in-person and remote attendance. If accepted would like to discuss with the workshop chairs the best practice and align with other workshops at FDG.

  
To support the workshop we would ideally use a flexible room that has video conferencing to allow for hybrid participation. A flexible layout would allow participants to break out into smaller groups and discussions during the workshop phases.
